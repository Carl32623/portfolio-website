Beyond the Gig: Investing in Real Employees

Why FedEx Should Invest in Its Employees Not Gig Drivers


October 2025 • 6-8 min read


The pitch for a gig-style last-mile model (think DoorDash/Uber-style independent contractors) is simple: variable labor costs and quick flex-up during peaks. But for FedEx—a premium, trust-anchored logistics brand—the long-term math favors building, training, and retaining FedEx-employed couriers. Here’s why the data supports staying employee-first.


Reliability beats “whoever’s available right now”

For parcels (vs. takeout), customers value reliability and accountability more than raw speed. A February 2025 McKinsey survey on U.S. e-commerce delivery found consumers optimize for reliability expectations by category and generally tolerate multi-day windows if the carrier is predictable—i.e., fewer misses, better communication, and consistent handoffs. That argues for trained, uniform standards executed by an aligned workforce, not a rotating pool of app contractors.


And when accountability is diffused across platforms and loosely vetted contractors, the risk profile changes. Recent enforcement and court actions around gig platforms underscore the gray zone in responsibility and controls, for example, San Francisco’s 2025 lawsuit against GoShare alleges misclassification in a scheduled-delivery model (Prop 22 may not shield pre-scheduled work).


Safety and liability risks rise in contractor-heavy delivery

Public data around contractor networks shows higher injury and safety concerns in last-mile and warehouse work broadly. The GAO and industry reports show serious-injury rates for warehouse and last-mile workers rose ~20–23% from 2018–2022 nationwide. That’s not a cost FedEx should externalize; it’s a risk to mitigate through training, coaching, and oversight—things easier to implement with employees.


Contractor ecosystems can also create fraud and control vulnerabilities. In 2025, a former DoorDash driver pleaded guilty in a federal case tied to a $2.5M delivery-system fraud that exploited platform and credential weaknesses—an example of how accountability gaps can become brand liabilities.


Engagement → better service → better economics (the service-profit chain)


This isn’t just theory. The service-profit chain—linking employee engagement to CX and profit—has decades of empirical support, and fresh data reinforces it. The U.K.’s Institute of Customer Service reported that every 1-point rise in employee engagement correlated with a 0.4-point rise in customer satisfaction, and firms with higher satisfaction saw meaningfully stronger revenue and EBITDA growth than laggards. That logic carries to parcel delivery: engaged couriers deliver fewer errors, better interactions at the door, and lower churn.


Gallup’s long-running meta-analyses similarly show engagement uplifts across quality, productivity, safety, and turnover—outcomes that directly affect last-mile consistency.


Turnover is expensive—especially for skilled routes

Replacing hourly/frontline talent is costly. SHRM reporting and HR benchmarks place hourly turnover near ~49% in many sectors, with replacement costs in the thousands per employee when you add recruiting, training, ramp time, and quality slippage during onboarding. Those costs quickly erase any “savings” from contractor models—especially when routes and customer familiarity matter.


The compliance “savings” can flip to liabilities

While California’s Prop 22 was upheld in 2024 for app-based rideshare/food delivery, it does not universally cover all scheduled logistics work—and misclassification exposure remains a live risk for delivery companies in other contexts and jurisdictions (with potential back pay, penalties, taxes). FedEx’s core business is package delivery; building around contractor status for that core could invite reclassification fights and reputational drag.


FedEx competes on trust. When customers see the FedEx uniform, truck, and scanner, they expect trained professionals operating under the Purple Promise. A contractor patchwork makes it harder to enforce standardized safety training, secure chain-of-custody, data privacy, and “make it right” remediation —all core to premium parcel service.


Internal cost-cutting is real—so invest cuts smartly (in people)

FedEx has been executing multi-year cost programs (DRIVE, Network 2.0), targeting $4B structural reductions by FY25 and incremental network savings by FY27. Those are good productivity moves—but the danger is if savings are extracted by quietly eroding employee value props. Research suggests that de-prioritizing employee experience ultimately raises hidden costs (turnover, safety incidents, re-deliveries, claims) and erodes CX.


FedEx’s FY25 Annual Report also shows the company still funds substantial retirement and postretiree benefits (e.g., $817M pension contributions in FY25; defined contribution plan expense $1.14B ), and it documents the structure of retiree health HRAs—closed to most new hires since 2018. When long-standing benefits narrow for incoming cohorts, the firm risks a two-tier culture that can dampen engagement.


  • Evidence of benefit tightening and workforce pressure
  • • Retiree medical closed to most new hires starting Jan 1, 2018 (shift to a capped HRA model documented in filings).
  • • Ongoing restructuring/layoffs in select regions: e.g., FedEx announced 1,700–2,000 job cuts in Europe to save ~$175M annually (2024). While sometimes necessary, serial cuts can undercut morale and service continuity if not offset by reinvestment in front-line capability.


Bottom line: if cost programs are paired with thinner benefits and fewer up-skilling opportunities, FedEx risks higher churn just as the network is being modernized—precisely when you need experienced couriers most.


Innovation lands better with an employed workforce

AI-assisted routing, EDT windows, dynamic densities—these only deliver value if front-line teams adopt and operationalize them. Employees are more likely than contractors to complete required training, follow SOPs, and contribute process improvements. FedEx’s own filings underscore major capital and tech bets; realizing their ROI requires stable teams.


What to do instead of a gig pivot

1. Double down on courier careers. Create clear pay progression, skills tiers (e.g., hazmat, cold-chain, complex multi-stop density), and micro-credentials that tie to raises/bonuses. (Correlates with higher engagement → higher CSAT → better revenue/EBITDA.)

2. Stabilize schedules and routes. Route familiarity reduces failed deliveries and claims; it also improves safety outcomes. (Serious-injury rates have risen in last-mile—training + stability matter.)

3. Protect and modernize benefits deliberately. Preserve signature elements (retirement match, medical plan quality), and clearly communicate any redesigns to avoid a “drip, drip” perception of takeaways that hurts retention. Use savings from DRIVE to fund front-line up-skilling and safety tech rather than eroding value props.

4. Measure the right outcomes. Tie ops scorecards to first-attempt delivery success, claims per 10k, injury rates, and customer NPS, not just drop density. Publish engagement and safety wins; investors increasingly view human-capital KPIs as predictors of quality growth.


Conclusion

The gig model may look cheaper on a spreadsheet, but the real-world costs—safety, quality, accountability, re-deliveries, fraud exposure, and turnover—stack up fast. The strongest evidence says invest in employees: engagement lifts customer satisfaction and financial performance, while stable, trained couriers safeguard the Purple Promise at the doorstep. FedEx should channel its transformation savings into the people who carry the brand—because in parcel delivery, trust travels in uniform.
