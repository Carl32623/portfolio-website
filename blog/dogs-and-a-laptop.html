Why I'm Finally Building a NAS

Aug 2025 • 5 min read


Earlier last week, I came home from a 10-hour work day dreaming about dinner, a shower, and my lazy chair. Instead, I walked into an active crime scene: two very happy dogs, one very unhappy side table, and a laptop that had just explored the concept of gravity.


Was this an accident? A coordinated enrichment activity? An unsanctioned QA stress test? All I know is there was a squeaky toy pronounced legally deceased, a tail operating at helicopter speeds, and my laptop doing its best impression of a mic drop. That one’s on me—I parked a computer on a launchpad disguised as a living room.


The Triage


Step one: push the power button like it owes me money. Nothing.
Step two: remember I have OneDrive backups.
Step three: discover that restoring a life’s worth of files from OneDrive feels like a boss level with 17 unskippable cutscenes.

Yes, the files returned. No, my sanity did not. There were version conflicts, partial syncs, and a folder that quantum-blinked in and out of existence like it was auditioning for a Marvel cameo.


Shiny New Laptop


On the bright side, I am now the new owner of an MSI Katana 17 Laptop. Top of the line? No. Do I have games to play? Absolutely (we’ll get into that at a later date). And it boots faster than it takes my dogs to realize I opened the cheese drawer. But this whole experience reminded me of a previous thought over topic: build a home NAS, so that my data lives on my network, in my home, with backups that don’t depend on the mood swings of a cloud.


The NAS Plan


Here’s the dream board:

  • Centralized storage for projects, school work, photos—anything I don’t want yeeted off a side table.
  • Automatic, versioned backups from laptops/phones (snapshots included, so I can undo my own heroics).
  • Redundancy (RAID) so a single drive failure is a pit stop, not a tow truck.
  • Local speed: 1 GbE minimum; 2.5 GbE if I’m feeling dangerous—restores shouldn’t feel like mailing a letter to myself.
  • Offsite copy every night to a cloud bucket—coverage for fires, floods, and dogs who learn to use tools.

The Setup...Hopefully


I’m keeping this simple and hard to break: a UGREEN 2-bay box with two matching NAS rated drives (4-8TB each) in a RAID1 mirror, because when a disk dies I want a coffee break, not a kitchen fire. I’ll add a 500 GB SSD for the OS and cache/app data (logs, packages, the nerdy bits that like fast I/O), hang it on 1–2.5 GbE, and back it with a small UPS so power disruptions don’t become data tragedy. What it’ll do: centralize projects/school/photos (maybe a movie here and there), run automatic versioned backups from laptops and phones, keep snapshots for easy rollbacks, and push a nightly encrypted offsite copy to the cloud. UGREEN boxes usually ship with their own OS, but I’m tempted to try a Linux distro (hello, Ubuntu) for the learning curve and flexibility.


Lessons Learned


  • Don’t store laptops on launchpads. Side tables next to a dog runway violates at least five OSHA rules.
  • Test restore, not just backups. If you haven’t practiced, you don’t have a plan.
  • Label everything for a confused Future You, because Future You will be confused.
  • Cloud is great...until it isn’t. It’s an excellent 2nd or 3rd copy solution. First copy should live on a box that you can touch.
  • Buy the rubber feet, straps, and cable management now. It may not be glamorous but can be widely effective. Gravity is undefeated.

The Dog Debrief


The suspects have been reprimanded with stern looks and several belly rubs. They have expressed deep remorse by immediately taking a nap shaped like a croissant. Justice served.
